How AI Changed Go: A Decade Review

Ten years after AlphaGo, Go did not simply become "AI Go". The record shows a new way of learning: moves query an oracle, patterns spread faster, and regional streams reorganize.

The page uses a structural lens: each move is read as chroma, where it falls in the game, crossed with rhythm, how it answers the board.

Three style streams through the AI decade

Decade read 3

Three style streams split, braid, and converge.

Before AlphaGo, the recorded Japan, Korea, and China streams sit apart. AlphaGo pulls them toward one public reference. Public engines then spread into local practice, and the streams separate again before settling into a closer oracle-era configuration.

The braid is the story: convergence is not permanent flattening. It is a sequence of shared shock, local absorption, and routine reference. Click each phase to reveal the readings behind the drawing.

Codex operation

AI turned Go knowledge into something players can query.

The important change is not that every player memorizes one AI move. It is that the community gains an external reference: a way to test candidate moves, reuse discoveries, and stabilize new habits faster. The archive becomes less like a rulebook and more like shared memory.

What this is not

A smaller claim is a stronger claim.

Not "AI did not matter"

It mattered by changing how moves are evaluated, how fast patterns circulate, and how regional streams stabilize.

Not a complete Go census

The analysis is about recorded CWI games and explicitly treats archive composition as part of the object.

Not national character

Regional labels refer to event streams in the archive, not claims about players or cultures as essences.

Not live engine analysis

The page uses precomputed aggregates and sampled KataGo traces so it can run as a static page.
